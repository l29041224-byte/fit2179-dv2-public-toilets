FIT2179 Data Visualisation 2

When Nature Calls

A data story about public toilet accessibility, pedestrian demand and inclusive facility gaps in Melbourne.

Vega-Lite maps City of Melbourne data Pedestrian demand Accessibility focus

Story focus

Public toilets are small pieces of infrastructure, but they affect how long people can comfortably stay in the city.

The main question is: are Melbourne’s public toilets located where people are likely to need them most, and do they support different users equally?

This data story moves from toilet supply, to accessibility gaps, to pedestrian demand, and finally to priority locations for closer checking.

The supply: Where are toilets and what do they provide?

The first part of the story looks at Melbourne’s public toilet network. It shows where toilets are located and whether they provide basic and inclusive facilities.

1. Where are the toilets?

This map shows public toilets recorded in the City of Melbourne dataset. The boundary shows the City of Melbourne municipal area. Each point is coloured by wheelchair accessibility.

2. What facilities are provided?

This chart counts toilets where each facility is recorded as available. It shows which facilities are common and which are less common. Baby-change facilities are much less available than basic male and female facilities.

The gap: Not every toilet supports every user

Counting toilets is not enough. A useful public toilet network also needs to support people with disability, families with children and different everyday needs.

3. Where are the access gaps?

This chart compares available, unavailable and unknown records. Baby-change facilities show the clearest gap. Wheelchair access is available in many locations, but not all.

Key insight: Baby-change facilities are the clearest inclusion gap in the current toilet dataset.

4. Is access keeping up with population?

This chart uses the current public toilet count. It compares this count with ABS population estimates for Melbourne LGA. It does not show historical toilet construction or yearly toilet counts. It shows how population growth can increase pressure on the existing network.

5. How inclusive is each toilet?

This lollipop chart counts how many selected facilities each toilet provides. Most toilets provide three selected features. Only a small number provide all four features.

6. Which types of places have more toilets?

This chart groups toilet names into simple place categories. Street locations and parks are the most common categories. This suggests that toilets support both daily movement and public recreation.

The demand: Are toilets close to busy places?

After looking at toilet supply and facility gaps, the story shifts to demand. Pedestrian sensor data helps show where public toilet need may be higher.

7. Where is likely demand highest?

This map uses pedestrian activity as a demand signal. Larger circles show busier pedestrian locations. The colour shows the distance to the nearest public toilet.

Why this matters: Public toilet demand is better understood by looking at where people move, not only where residents live.

8. How far are busy places from a toilet?

This lollipop chart ranks busy pedestrian locations by distance to the nearest toilet. Shorter distance suggests better immediate access. Longer distance suggests locations that may need closer checking.

The decision: Where should attention go first?

The final step combines demand and access. Locations with high pedestrian activity and longer distance to toilets become possible priority areas for closer review.

9. Which locations should be checked first?

This quadrant chart compares pedestrian demand with nearest toilet distance. Locations with high demand and longer distance are potential priority areas. This helps move the visualisation from description to decision support.

Decision focus: Locations with high pedestrian activity and longer toilet distance should be checked first.

10. Are busy places also inclusive?

This heatmap checks whether busy locations have inclusive toilet access nearby. It considers toilets within 300 metres of each pedestrian location. Baby-change access is more limited than general toilet access.

Final takeaway

Melbourne’s public toilet network is visible across central activity areas, but access quality varies by facility type.

Baby-change facilities are the clearest inclusion gap in this dataset.

Pedestrian demand adds another layer. Busy locations should be checked not only for nearby toilets, but also for inclusive features.

The main recommendation is to prioritise high-demand places where walking distance is longer or inclusive access is limited.

Method note

Pedestrian counts are used as a proxy for likely public toilet demand.

This does not prove exact toilet need, because demand can also depend on tourists, events, opening hours and individual user needs.

Distance to the nearest toilet is used as an accessibility indicator. However, it does not include walking barriers, toilet condition or whether a toilet is open at a specific time.

The purpose of this visualisation is to identify areas for closer review, not to make a final infrastructure decision.
